A Specific Theory of Sovereign AI

Industrial Strategy as Early-stage Venture

By Lawrence Lundy-Bryan

OpenAI just bought 6 gigawatts of compute capacity with AMD, matching the UK’s entire national AI infrastructure ambition through 2030. I know right.

They’ve got another 10 gigawatts in their back pocket from Nvidia and committed roughly $1 trillion to infrastructure buildout. A single American company is deploying more capital into AI than the UK’s entire annual R&D budget across all sectors. Yikes. The Sun Never Set on the British Empire etc etc.

The UK will not compete on compute scale. Jonno and I argued we need to think about compute as a datacentre and edge spectrum and be smart about where to invest. I got even more specific in my compound semi piece, saying the UK needs to lead where it has structural advantages. But it’s fair to argue that picking specific companies (IQE?) or technology platforms (GaN, InP?) gets a bit British Leyland. Leave it to the market to pick winners. Government doesn’t have the information markets have about what will actually work commercially.

The UK already funds undirected R&D. Not enough. And too slowly. But it funds it. Scientists get cash through research councils to do curiosity-driven research (mostly), and that works, publish papers, train PhDs, advance knowledge. At the other end of the spectrum is the market, and society thinks price is the right signal to allocate capital toward commercialisation. VC backs startups, later-stage investors scale winners, market discipline weeds out what doesn’t work. It works so well. No wonder our economy is booming. Amiright?

Somewhere in the middle sits Innovate UK giving grants to bridge the gap, supporting commercialisation of research that’s too applied for research councils but too early for pure commercial funding. This made sense when technology development timelines were measured in years. It still makes sense for those technologies. Taking 18 months to approve a manufacturing innovation grant was fine because the underlying technology wasn’t moving that fast. Think Fusion or Quantum Computing. Oh, hold on…

But AI broke the model. The technology moves on 3-6 month cycles. Moore’s Law for AI agents is 7 months per doubling.

The strategic positions that matter: who sets the standard for privacy-preserving AI, who creates the essential datasets, and who demonstrates safe deployment in critical applications, are being claimed right now.

By the time traditional mechanisms deploy capital, it’s already too late.

I can’t stress enough how important this is.

We are moving too slowly.

So where exactly should industrial strategy sit when the constraint isn’t knowing what to fund, but moving fast enough that the funding matters? I think there’s a specific answer for AI that doesn’t require government to pick winners in the traditional sense….

Building an Institution for Speed

The fundamental challenge isn’t knowing what to fund. It’s moving fast enough that decisions matter. The first country solving privacy-preserving AI at scale sets the global standard. The first comprehensive dataset in a domain becomes what everyone builds on. Windows of strategic opportunity open and close in months.

Traditional government funding operates on 18-month cycles (at best). Research councils issue calls, proposals go through peer review, committees debate, funding gets staged over years. Milestone based. Onerous reporting. Founders spending time writing grant updates not speaking to customers. This works for fundamental science where timelines span decades. It fails catastrophically for strategic technology where timescales are quarters. By the time conventional mechanisms deploy capital, standards have locked in and winners selected.

The Sovereign AI Unit or any other institution must make decisions in 2 weeks (why not 1 week?) and deploy capital in 2-4 weeks. This isn’t about efficiency. It’s about whether investments arrive in time in go out and win fast-moving markets. But operating at this speed with taxpayer money inverts every normal government instinct. Every standard practice pushes toward more review, more process, more consultation. The temptation is to act as custodians protecting public funds from waste. There is always a reason to say no or to do more diligence or to ask for more data. That’s growth investing and good luck to you. God speed.

But this moment is different. Even if you’re timelines are longer than mine. Even if you think this is a financial bubble, and we should just hold our horses. The theory of failure isn’t investing in the wrong things and losing money. It’s being too slow to invest in the right things and missing the window where those investments create value. SovAI could fund 10 projects and 8 fail but 2 capture global leadership positions, that’s success. If it funds ten projects and all deliver solid research but arrived too late to shape standards, that’s failure even though the money was “well spent” by traditional metrics.

Two Market Failures Worth Funding

With institutional foundations established, what actually gets funded? I think the right level of abstraction is to focus on two specific market failures.

Market Failure 1: Privacy-Preserving AI and Data Infrastructure

Yes I’m on this one again. Won’t stop, can’t stop. Because I learned from the BritCard announcement and reaction that people don’t know privacy technologies now exist and work pretty well. Hear me: We can analyse data AND not reveal sensitive data. We can safely store sensitive information on computers, right. We can send sensitive data around the Internet. We now have the tools to process data safely too.

But we basically don’t do it yet. Why? I’ve puzzled on this one for a while. Startups have gone around pitching fancy multi-party computation, zero-knowledge proofs, federated learning, and more recently fully-homomorphic encryption. You want acryonms to make it easier, sir? Try MPC, ZKP, FL, FHE, PQC, TEE, and DP. All of these techniques improve every month and become easier to use. And yet, despite the obvious benefits in finance, healthcare, and government, we’ve seen little take up.

It’s a market failure driven by risk aversion and regulation creating a catch-22. Organisations with sensitive data—NHS trusts, government departments, financial institutions—are incredibly risk-averse about adopting new technologies, especially when those technologies involve complicated cryptographic math that procurement teams don’t understand. GDPR and compliance regulations have actually made this worse: the higher the stakes around data protection, the harder it becomes to buy novel solutions because proving compliance for homomorphic encryption or differential privacy to a risk committee is much harder than just not collecting the data at all. Meanwhile, the companies building privacy-preserving tech can’t get customers because everyone’s waiting for someone else to go first. And VCs won’t fund continued development because there’s no clear path to revenue when your potential customers are paralyzed by procurement risk. Classic chicken-and-egg where the technology exists, the need is real, but the gap won’t close without intervention.

The UK is uniquely positioned to lead here. We have some of the world’s best datasets that other countries can’t replicate: UK Biobank holds genetic and health data on half a million participants with decades of follow-up, creating longitudinal health insights no-one can match. NHS holds health records at true population scale—70 million people, cradle to grave, in a single “integrated” system that fragmented US healthcare can never achieve. The Met Office has century-spanning climate observations with unprecedented spatial and temporal resolution. Financial transaction data from the City of London captures global capital flows that reveal systemic risks invisible to any single institution.

These datasets are currently underutilized because organizations are terrified of privacy violations. But if the UK demonstrates that privacy-preserving technologies enable researchers to train AI models on UK Biobank data without ever seeing individual genetic sequences, or allows international climate researchers to run computations on Met Office observations without raw data leaving UK servers, or lets financial regulators analyze systemic risk using encrypted transaction data where no single analyst can see individual trades—we establish both technical feasibility and regulatory frameworks that become the global standard. Public trust in institutions like NHS means citizens will share data if privacy is genuinely protected. World-class cryptography research at Cambridge, Oxford, and Imperial provides the technical foundation.

If the UK cracks privacy-preserving AI in practice—not just research papers but actual deployment—it sets the global standard for how democracies develop AI ethically. Other nations will adopt UK frameworks because we demonstrated it’s possible to have both comprehensive data and strong privacy. The UK becomes essential to global AI development not just because of valuable datasets, but because we solved the fundamental constraint preventing others from collecting similar data. Every democracy facing pressure to both advance AI and protect privacy will look to UK frameworks.

The investment strategy: fund systematic development of privacy-preserving techniques (£50-75M), then fund creation of comprehensive datasets using those techniques in domains where the UK has advantages (£125-150M for biomedical data building on UK Biobank and NHS, climate data through Met Office, scientific data across domains where UK research leads). Privacy tech isn’t separate from data infrastructure. It’s how the UK achieves data leadership.

Speed matters because this is a race to set standards. Multiple companies are working on privacy-preserving AI. The first to demonstrate it working at population scale in high-stakes domains like healthcare establishes the framework everyone else adopts. If approval takes 18 months and someone else demonstrates it first, the leadership position is permanently lost.

Market Failure 2: Early-Stage Hard-Tech AI Research

More controversial. But there’s a widely held belief that VC funds early-stage AI research. This was maybe true five years ago but is definitely false today. The AI venture market has evolved into something that looks more like late-stage growth investing. VCs are funding hot deals with momentum, clear paths to next rounds, and social proof from other investors, not genuinely uncertain technical research. I will fight anyone who tells me otherwise.

VC returns are power-law distributed, so funds need huge winners to make economics work. In the current AI environment, VCs believe huge winners require massive scale, which requires raising huge subsequent rounds at higher valuations. This creates extreme pressure to fund companies that can obviously raise Series A, not companies doing important but uncertain technical work. A partner who backs genuinely novel research that takes 18 months to reach results can’t defend that investment when competitors are funding “safer” deals that can raise markup rounds immediately. Behold my ARR growth! No-one ever got fired for buying a ex-Deepmind founder. I can tell you this plainly because the first question you really ask when you see a seed company is: what does the A investor need to see to fund this?

This creates a real funding gap for work that’s too applied for research councils (trying to build something commercial, not publish papers) but too early and uncertain for current venture dynamics (no clear path to revenue in 12-18 months, genuine technical risk rather than just execution risk). This is exactly the work that often matters most strategically: novel architectures that might be superior to transformers, fundamentally different approaches to training, AI capabilities for defense or critical infrastructure that have small markets but high strategic value (compound semi). Or my pet favourite, temporal computing chips.

The UK needs to fund this gap. The unit should fund teams with genuinely promising technical research on AI capabilities that matter strategically but cannot raise venture capital because the work is too uncertain or the markets too specialized:

  • Defence and intelligence: Autonomous systems, intelligence analysis, cyber capabilities. Small addressable markets relative to consumer AI, so VCs won’t fund them even though strategic value is enormous.

  • Critical infrastructure: AI for energy grid management, transport systems, communications infrastructure. These require operational reliability and safety guarantees that consumer AI doesn’t prioritize.

  • Healthcare delivery at NHS scale: Medical AI applications that work in the UK’s healthcare system with its specific constraints and data characteristics. Can’t just be US models adapted—requires ground-up development for British context.

  • Novel technical approaches: Alternative architectures beyond transformers, new training paradigms, approaches to efficiency and edge deployment that reduce dependence on centralized compute. Most will fail, but the optionality is strategically valuable.

The common thread is work that VCs would have funded in 2019 but won’t fund now because their capital has moved toward lower-risk, higher-certainty deals. A talented team at pre-seed doing genuinely novel work on autonomous systems or medical imaging is exactly what government fast funding should support—promising enough that experts immediately see value, but too early for venture capital that needs clear paths to large markets and follow-on funding.

Inventors, scientists and entrepreneurs need to know that there is a fast-moving pot of capital available for them to do important work. Not just work that will get them a grant. Not just work that will get them VC funding.

The fast funding model is essential because timing determines outcomes. These researchers aren’t thinking about specific applications yet, they’re focused on making technology work. Traditional funding takes 18 months, by which time they’ve either joined a commercial lab, taken what VC money they can get (pushing them toward consumer applications), or relocated to the US. Funding them in 2 weeks while they’re doing R&D, making introductions to institutions like NHS or MOD who can articulate requirements, shapes development toward strategic applications before other incentives lock in different directions.

This isn’t charity for researchers who can’t raise venture. It’s strategic investment in capabilities the UK needs but markets won’t fund because venture dynamics have shifted toward momentum rather than technical risk-taking.

Why These Two Bets Work Together

Privacy-preserving AI enables data collection at scales others cannot match while maintaining democratic values, establishing UK global leadership in how AI development happens ethically. And early-stage hard-tech funding builds capabilities in strategic applications where domestic access is needed and where genuine technical innovation is happening but can’t access capital.

Together they create a coherent position: the UK leads on enabling global AI development through privacy infrastructure, and demonstrates excellence in deploying AI in high-stakes applications that matter for security, energy, healthcare, and critical infrastructure. Both require velocity because leadership comes from being first. Both address clear market failures where private capital systematically underprovides despite high strategic value.

What Success Looks Like in 18 Months

  • Institutional proof points: 80-100 investment decisions with two-week cycle times. Portfolio includes 15-20 clear failures that demonstrate appropriate risk-taking. Team operating at “venture” speed (US-style, not EU-style, because EU is slow and bad, amiright?)

  • Privacy and data leadership: UK has deployed privacy-preserving AI in at least two high-stakes domains (likely NHS data and another). International working groups on AI privacy are using UK frameworks as starting points. At least three major AI companies are engaging with UK privacy infrastructure for their models.

  • Strategic applications: Funded 30-40 teams working on defense, infrastructure, or healthcare AI for which the venture case was weak because of market size or timeline. At least 5-7 have demonstrated capabilities that government institutions want to deploy.

18 months is too early to claim victory on global leadership, but it’s the right timeframe to demonstrate the institution works and that it’s on trajectory.

The Core Argument

  • The UK needs an institution, ideally the Sovereign AI Unit, built to move with speed and conviction.

  • An acceptance of high failure rates as necessary for high impact.

  • Concentrate investment in areas where market failures create opportunities for strategic government funding.

  • Privacy-preserving AI

  • Early-stage AI adjacent hard-tech research.

  • Both require velocity because leadership comes from being first.

  • Both position the UK for strategic leadership in global AI development.

This only works if the institution is designed for speed from day one. Everything else flows from that foundational choice.
