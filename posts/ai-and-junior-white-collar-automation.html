AI and Junior White Collar Automation: Update after EIG’s New Report

12 Aug 2025
10 min read
What if I am wrong? How will I live with myself? Who will look after my family?

So here we all are then. “It’s So Over” I guess? Up and down we go like clockwork. Hype to raise the money and get attention. Backlash when hype doesn’t match reality. But, come on guys, it happens everytime, why does everyone continue to fall for it? Speculative cycles are central to the deployment of new technologies.

It’s how this whole thing works. There is a whole framework on it that continues to hold up well: “Frenzy phase: Increased speculation and financialization leads to a decoupling between financial capital and production capital. Capital is invested more in financial innovations than in technological innovations. Asset bubbles are inflated.”

Hold steady, lads. Ignore all the takes. And behold the data:

Does the straight line on the chart continue to go up and to the right? Yes. Will progress from here get harder? Yes. Case closed.

Well, look, we’re getting much better at building AI agents that can work reliably for tasks lasting tens of minutes, but getting them to work for hours without mistakes is a tougher ask. Getting further up and to the right, basically means tracking progress across 4 key areas (i’m probably missing others). There is nothing I learned from the release that told me progress has stopped. Slowed down, maybe. But I don’t see a wall? I really don’t see any evidence here that categorically says LLMs won’t scale to AGI.

Now the race is on to increase get agents to be more useful by successfully completing longer tasks. The chatter suggests 4 key areas to watch:

  1. Compounding errors: In long tasks, small mistakes pile up until the whole process fails. Reflexion (iterative self-correction), Tree-of-Thought (exploring multiple reasoning paths), and self-consistency (picking the most common answer from several tries) help catch and fix these mistakes before they snowball. This lets agents recover mid-task and complete more steps without breaking down.

  2. Memory and state: Agents lose track when they can’t hold enough context in memory. Moving from RAG (retrieve relevant info before answering) to GraphRAG (store and link information as a graph) makes it easier to keep track of facts and relationships over time. Testing with very long contexts (200k–2M tokens) ensures accuracy and speed hold up for hours, not just minutes.

  3. Planning — Quick wins are possible with reactive prompting, but lasting performance needs a clear plan-then-execute approach. In robotics and other operational settings, affordance-aware planning (e.g., SayCan) checks that each action is possible in the real world. This avoids dead ends and wasted steps, keeping the task on track across many decisions.

  4. Verification — Even small errors can spread if not caught. Chain-of-Verification (CoVe) and SelfCheck build in regular fact and code checks, increasing the share of correct, evidence-backed outputs. Continuous verification keeps long processes from quietly drifting off course.

So there you go. It’s still happening. But it will probably take *slightly* longer, and it will be harder.

AI and The Missing Junior White Collar Automation

But also bad news for me yesterday, it turns out I might be wrong? A sad day indeed.

My working thesis has been that AI will hit white-collar graduates first. The Economic Innovation Group’s (EIG) new analysis, “AI and Jobs: The Final Word (Until the Next One)”, basically says: there is no junior automation.

What gives?

Well, the EIG sliced the U.S. labour market by multiple measures of AI exposure and found no broad displacement of the most-exposed workers. In fact, the most-exposed quintile remains better paid, more educated, and has lower unemployment than the least-exposed quintile.

From 2022 to early 2025, unemployment rose 0.30 percentage points for the most-exposed group versus 0.94 points for the least-exposed. They also find no spike in labour-force exits or in switching away from AI-exposed occupations. Interesting and counter-intuitive.

So, taken literally, my thesis fails a strict read of today’s aggregates. But a *closer* look, as always, shows the “white-collar-first” view is not wrong. Excuse me a little post hoc rationalization if you will.

It just needs to be reframed to where the impact shows up first: the entry pipeline and the task mix, not just headcount. Yet. And the “Yet” continues to do a lot of heavy lifting.

What EIG actually says

EIG ran the same checks several different ways: unemployment by exposure quintile, probability of leaving the labour force, occupational switching, and a sector lens where AI use is higher. Across these, the story is: no large, economy-wide AI job losses yet among AI-exposed workers.

Where there are small differences, they are modest and could reflect the composition of these workers rather than AI causality. The authors emphasize the main visible change today is in tasks rather than jobs. They note official firm-adoption is still low (about 9% of firms report using AI in production as of mid-2025), with adoption concentrated in information sectors, and that many firms report replacing some tasks but not reducing net headcount. 9% is either quite high of quite low depending on how much kool aid you’ve drunk.

These findings are consistent with other data. The Federal Reserve’s review of AI uptake finds worker-reported use is much higher than firm-reported “in-production” use, which implies a diffusion lag between capability at the desk and reorganization at the firm.

As I wrote in Unbundling The Job: “The classic example is how early factories initially just replaced steam engines with electric motors but kept the same centralised power distribution system, long belts and shafts running throughout the building from a single power source. This provided some benefits but didn't fundamentally change how work was organized. The real transformation came when factories redesigned around electric motors' unique advantage: you could put individual motors at each workstation."

This lag, or lack of reorganisation, is, in my view, why we haven’t seen layoffs.

Where the thesis still holds

If we look where early effects should appear first, the data line up with the intuition that LLMs replace the junior rung of the ladder. I am mainly tracking entry-level openings, new-grad hiring and internships.

  • Entry-level postings have fallen sharply. Revelio found more than 35% fewer entry-level openings since January 2023, or over 100,000 fewer new postings per month. That is where you would expect “AI does junior tasks” to show up first. Fewer postings for grads is a hit to new cohorts even if incumbents keep their jobs.

  • Internships (US & UK): The internship market, imo the cleanest leading indicator of the junior pipeline has cooled. In the US, Handshake shows postings down about 15% since early 2023, with applications rising (41% of the Class of 2025 had applied). NACE’s 2025 survey found net intern hiring is projected to fall 3.1%, with offer and conversion rates at multi-year lows. In the UK, Adzuna tracks ~30% fewer advertised internships than in 2022, aligning with a broader ~32% drop in entry-level roles.

  • New-grad hiring (National Association of Colleges and Employers)(NACE): Employers cut hiring for the Class of 2024 by 5.8%. For the Class of 2025, they initially projected a 7.3% increase, citing talent-pipeline, etc. By spring 2025 plans were revised down to just 0.6% growth. Effectively flat. In other words, over the course of 2024, many employers scaled back the stronger rebound they had expected. Important signal? Maybe.

Pair these with EIG’s finding that unemployment is rising for young workers in general, regardless of exposure, I think we have a more nuanced picture: employers are *currently* keeping staff and augmenting them with AI, but they hire fewer at the bottom. That does not register as displacement of “AI-exposed workers” in the aggregate yet, because entrants are a small share of the workforce and the exposure measures are built around current jobholders, not positions a firm didn’t post.

Reconciling anecdotes with aggregates

“Jeff Bezos: “I have a saying which is: When the data and the anecdotes disagree, the anecdotes are usually right.”

That’s a great quote to use if you don’t want to change your mind in the face of data. And Jeff is famous and successful and so we should listen to him right?

But why does the EIG picture feel at odds with daily experience of LLMs doing tasks previously done by junior staff? Well:

  1. Task substitution before job substitution. EIG found that firms report replacing tasks more than workers. Managers keep teams intact while redistributing work around AI. That shows up as productivity gain and scope expansion for staff rather than immediate layoffs. Slowly and then all at once?

  2. Diffusion is uneven and slow at the firm level. 9% of firms report production use. And ChatGPT has ~700M weekly users globally. Square that circle. Until informal use is integrated into governed workflows with budgets, controls, and SLAs, it won’t show up as firm-level adoption or headcount effects.

  3. Cohort masking in aggregates. New graduates are a tiny slice of the workforce. So even big swings in their outcomes barely nudge the national unemployment rate, which is dominated by everyone else. EIG explicitly warns about this: because new entrants are a small portion of the labor force, you shouldn’t infer their situation from aggregate exposure buckets—you need to look at them directly

Learning from the data: a revised thesis

Time to be more specific:

Near term (2025–2027): AI compresses the junior ladder in AI-exposed functions. The first impact is on entry-level hiring and postings, not on headline employment. Teams get smaller over time through attrition and slower backfilling rather than layoffs.

Medium term (2027–2030): As firm-level adoption moves from experimentation to standardized workflows (reorg the firm around electric motors), hiring bars rise, promotion velocity slows, and total white-collar headcount in exposed functions plateaus. This is consistent with EIG’s finding of modest effects today, plus early pipeline data pointing down.

What signals I’m watching

  1. Firm AI adoption and hiring outlook: From the U.S. Census Bureau’s Business Trends and Outlook Survey (BTOS), I watch the share of firms reporting AI in production (currently 9%), and their six-month headcount expectations. If adoption moves well above 10–15% and the share expecting headcount to fall rises at the same time, that would indicate AI shifting impact from individual tasks to whole jobs.

  2. Entry-level job postings: Monthly data from Revelio and Indeed on openings for junior roles in software, data, design, customer operations, and generalist analyst positions. Persistent declines, especially in occupations with high AI exposure, would point to a shrinking entry-level talent pipeline.

  3. Graduate hiring trends: NACE surveys on the Class of 2025–2026 and career-services placement rates. If these drop while overall employment stays stable, it would signal fewer opportunities for new graduates.

  4. Promotion rates in tech: HR datasets tracking promotion velocity and backfill rates. A slowdown would suggest career ladders are flattening before layoffs happen. I plan to cross-check this with function-level job posting trends to confirm the signal.

What did we all learn here

EIG shows that broad displacement of AI-exposed white-collar workers is not yet in the data. Fair.

My thesis survives in a refined form: AI is first reducing demand for new white-collar entrants and junior tasks, with the aggregate effects deferred by firm-level inertia and the substitution of tasks before jobs.

If the leading indicators move together, the cohort-level squeeze will eventually surface in the aggregates.
