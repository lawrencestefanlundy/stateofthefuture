Fusion & nuclear

AI for Nuclear Fusion (Feat. Martin Riedmiller, Google DeepMind)

27 Jan 2025
14 min read
AI is going to change everything. Will it accelerate nuclear fusion?

Hello, welcome one and all.

When the scale-pilled think about powering out 100GW datacentres, obviousy the short-term answer is SMRs (also gas, let’s be honest, it will be gas, but we can’t talk about that). The long-term answer is fusion. In Nuclear Fusion: The State of Play I wrote:

…commercial fusion before 2035 remains unlikely. I’m increasing the probability from 5% to 15%, but significant technical and economic hurdles suggest deployment is more realistic in the 2040+ timeframe. The biggest opportunities imo are AI for Plasma Control Systems, Digital Twin/Simulation Software, and Advanced Materials and Component Manufacturing especially for Rare-earth barium copper oxide (ReBCO) tape manufacturing, first wall materials, and ICF target cost reductions.

Some argue we will crack this sooner. Who am I to bet against Sam Altman and US ability to “just do things”. I mean AI for material science is another thread to pull. ++ quantum for materials/chemistry is a viable candidate for practical quantum computers.

So I ask: what are the actual rate limiting issues in fusion?

Most of you would have seen the news a while ago that DeepMind put it’s algos to the task of containing plasma “Accelerating fusion science through learned plasma control”. If we can control plasma, we knock down one of the major hurdles of commercial fusion.

So I spoke with Google DeepMind's Research Director Martin Riedmiller about his team's breakthrough in fusion reactor control. Our conversation explores the implications for complex control systems and his work combining LLMs with reinforcement learning, offering insights into the future of not just plasma containment but more broadly robotics and autonomous control systems.

Fusion. Plasma. Robotics. LLMs. RL. What’s not to like? Read on…

I learned:

  1. RL is uniquely positioned for complex plasma control problems - The success with tokamak control wasn't tokamak-specific. The techniques could potentially extend to other plasma control challenges like semiconductor etching or space propulsion. What makes this particularly significant is that these systems are often too complex for traditional control methods but share similar underlying physics and control challenges. Riedmiller's work suggests we might be able to develop generalized RL control approaches for the broader field of plasma physics applications.

  2. The future of industrial control systems may be "plug and play" - A revelation from the interview is Riedmiller's vision of a "universal controller" that can adapt to any sensor input or actuator output, similar to how the human brain can adapt to different sensory conditions. While we're not there yet, his team's work demonstrates progress toward control systems that can automatically adapt to different hardware configurations and control objectives without specialized reprogramming. This could revolutionize how we approach industrial automation.

  3. LLMs as World Models for RL - Instead of the standard approach of using RL to improve LLMs, Riedmiller is flipping the script: using LLMs as "pre-trained world models" to make RL more efficient. By leveraging LLMs' encoded knowledge of how the world works (like "to grasp something, get close first"), robots can learn new tasks with far less trial and error. This suggests a future where AI systems combine the rapid learning of human-like common sense with the precision of machine control.

Interview

Q: Hey Martin, why are you interested in fusion containment as a problem to solve?

A: My personal subject is reinforcement learning for control of dynamical systems, pushing the boundary of what's possible with RL in control. I've been working on this since my PhD. Making it more data efficient is really important - so you can learn from as few data points as possible. Like humans are able to see something, digest it, make a model, use that model to refine the policy, do a few control steps in the real world and then rethink. We push the boundary quite a bit on that front.

We came across fusion as a problem and thought this is interesting because you have a large action space with 19 magnetic coils that you want to control simultaneously. This gives you many possibilities to have a control action at any point in time, which makes the RL problem pretty hard. Then as a second thought, this could help humanity advance because it's an interesting field like fusion. So on one side, my interest was pushing the frontier of what's possible with RL control. On the other side, the fusion researchers we collaborated with at EPFL had all the knowledge about fusion. We said, what can we do together that's not easy to achieve with classical control methods? That was the motivation.

Q: Was the project driven by an analysis of the fusion approach most likely to achieve commercial fusion?

A: The problem was posed by the Swiss Plasma Center as an interesting and high impact problem to solve. I'm not an expert in fusion, I only learned that along the way, but we asked them what was an interesting problem in that domain. There's research in general about plasmas and understanding what you can do with them. Then there's another field about getting energy out of a fusion reactor, which is a slightly different beast. Our thinking was: if we can do something useful for them that replaces what they can do with their current methods, this will raise interest and open the field for us. We can do the first steps and see what we can do there. Then look at what else is needed - are there interesting problems we can help solve by learning, which cannot be solved by classical methods? That would be the ideal case.

Q: Why did you choose a LSTM-based critic and a feedforward-based actor architecture? Was it designed to generalize across broader control problems?

A: The standard case would be to use recurrency or LSTMs in both the actor and critic. That would be the natural choice because observations usually only reflect a point in time. We know from control theory that you need the derivatives or need to go back further in the past to have a full state of the system. An easy example is if you have only the position of a car, you don't know how to brake because it depends on the velocity. You can only observe velocity if you have access to previous time steps. With an LSTM, you can build that knowledge within the network.

Why we ended up with this actor-critic was because we had heavy time constraints - we were working at 10 kilohertz. Bringing this speed to the real world was already a challenge. We didn't manage to fully transport the LSTMs to that level because of time restrictions. We really compiled the system to run quickly on their target hardware. And doing it with LSTMs, we just didn't manage to meet the time constraints. Then we figured out a feedforward-only controller was enough, so we took this practical approach.

Q: So for a version 2, with more time and money could you implement the LSTM on different hardware?

A: Yes, it's basically an engineering task to make an LSTM work on their target hardware or to do it with an attention mechanism and have a longer context. It's an engineering task rather than a general unsolvable problem. We saw that it worked, and this was already a lot of work, so for that project, we stopped there.

Q: You've demonstrated success for tokamak magnetic control. Is the architecture Tokamak specific? What key modifications are required for other complex control problems?

A: The underlying system is completely general. That's what we're working in our team to understand - the general principles. The systems might vary. Sometimes you have observations that are very close to a state, then you don't need recurrences. Sometimes you need a lot of understanding of the past, then you have to work more on bringing this into the system so it can learn a state representation. If you have fewer action dimensions, like if you only want to control 3 coils instead of 19, the reinforcement learning problem becomes less difficult for the learning controller.

You could take this system and control a water tank or chemical reactors. We controlled airplanes in simulation to show the system can learn - the underlying control system and approach is very general. What is specific is how you phrase your reinforcement learning problem. For shape control, it's not just reaching a velocity - you want a certain shape on the plasma. We solved that by having certain points you want to match as closely as possible, having a hull around the plasma and defining the errors with respect to the shape. That's more specific to the problem, the reward design. But as soon as you have the basic principle of how the RL controller works, how the learning method works, that is very general.

Q: The field has moved away from LSTMs towards transformers. Could the fusion containment architecture be transformer-based? Or could this LSTM actor-critic approach be a general model for control tasks?

A: That's not decided. My guess is that because there's so much work on attention mechanisms and so many tricks, it will probably become the standard architecture. In general, it's difficult to say from a theoretical perspective. If you have general recurrence, you can go really far back in the past, and by learning, it figures out the right ways. With a context window, you have to decide how big it is and it's more expensive to compute.

The important thing is understanding that we need some way of going from observations to a state-like representation - state in the sense of a Markov decision process, where given that state information and action, you can predict the probability for the successor state. This isn't given if you only take the observation. In some cases, like with a car, you can say "I know velocity plays a role, so I just compute the velocity externally, take this as input - I don't need LSTMs or attention." That's the simplest system. But for more complicated systems where you have less knowledge of how the physics pans out, you might want to work with the most general system possible. The answer depends on the implementation level.

Q: Your point about efficiency is a good one. Infinite context will be computationally tractable but maybe not economically. Could this be a specific model to solve this problem cost effectively? What about other plasma control problems like semiconductor etching or space propulsion?

A: We want a general controller that, like a human brain, no matter what senses you attach to it and what actors you attach to it, will figure out just by having this reward function implemented. Like how people can use their body if they only have one eye or are deaf. That's our final goal. Theoretically, you want a system that effectively understands what standards you provide are useful, comes up with a latent representation in the middle, and from there goes to control.

In practice, you always have efficiency in mind. In robotics, we usually work with raw camera signals. We want the system not only to understand how to grasp an object if the position is known, but really from the pixels of the camera. All sensor signals come in, and the system has to make sense of them. There's a trade-off - the more precise information you present to solve the problem, the faster the system can learn. If you give more raw data, it spends effort figuring out the right representation.

Q: Are you exploring event-based cameras and neuromorphic sensors for this? Would you want to do some pre-processing at the sensor level to reduce communication and processing costs?

A: At the moment, we're just using standard digital cameras because they're the easiest to access, but having event-based sensors and neuromorphic cameras would be an interesting extension. Ideally, in this vision of having a brain, you just attach whatever sensor you have and observe: does the brain learn fast or slow, can it make use of it? That's definitely an interesting research direction, but we're currently not going that way. We just want to have the hardware as standardized as possible.

Q: What is the extension of the original research? How are you improving the system?

A: We are trying to bring down the sample complexity by having better exploration methods. We're using LLMs to introduce real-world knowledge. For example, one way to learn to grasp is to simply provide a reward function and say "whenever the object is lifted, you get a reward." But then the robot explores all kinds of useless stuff because it doesn't know what grasping means. Now with LLMs, you already have an idea that to grasp something, you have to get close, and then you can have an intermediate reward. That helps do better exploration. This is what we're mainly exploring with my team.

Q: That makes me think of LLMs as a universal knowledge platform. Plug in different algorithms and systems and use it as a universal knowledge base. It's not quite an operating system but has some similarities.

A: That's a good picture. In our case, we have a scene we're observing with cameras. You want the system to give hints about the scenes. Provide the task parameters and sensor data and ask how the system would solve it. Then you get hints, and you can transform these hints into several parts of a reinforcement learning controller to make RL more data efficient.

Q: It's interesting when you say data efficiency because the LLM is the opposite. It's the least data efficient model I can imagine. We use it to reduce the search space, and then top search more efficiently.

A: Exactly. These LLMs are fed with a lot of compiled knowledge - the knowledge we wrote in books. The overall process was not at all data efficient, but humans were able to collect all this experience and compress it into something that now allows generalization in language. But what you finally want to do to understand intelligence is go directly from the raw experience, the input data.

Q: Are there other interesting areas of research that might yield useful results in the RL and control space?

A: One interesting direction is imitation learning - a human showing a robot how to do things or controlling the robot. Taking this knowledge, like in language models, and hoping it will generalize to new robots and environments. I'm a bit skeptical because I'm not sure how much data you'd need until you see generalization effects. But if this worked, it would help boost reinforcement learning because you'd have a controller that already works with a suboptimal solution. Then the task would be to collect data to refine that policy or get to the optimum.

Q: In your working conversations with colleagues at EPFL, have you shortened your timelines for a practical nuclear reactor?

A: I'm not a fusion expert, but what I've learned is there are fundamental physics questions to be solved to make fusion happen, beyond the control problem. I would be really happy if we can contribute on the control side by providing a reinforcement learning controller that can deal with certain control problems you can't put in a classical framework. If you have a reasonable simulator, you can train a reinforcement learning controller and accelerate your research. Also opening the eyes of control engineers doing fusion that there's something beyond PID control, beyond linear control, that can help make fusion 10% better or whatever it is. That's where I see the role of reinforcement learning.

Q: As someone working in RL for so long, do you think RL will make a comeback and dominate frontier AI progress in the next 5 years in these large reasoning models like O3?

A: I'm skeptical that LLMs are the final answer. It's like an optical illusion. Sometimes I look at it and say, well, the only thing we've learned from LLMs is that big data and big networks generalize in an unexpected way. That's fascinating, but not more. Then I see a new application coming in - wow. But I still think that to really understand intelligence, this low level work we're focusing on with RL control - going from raw experience to something you can solve, then finding a name for it, bringing it to a higher level with context - that's what LLMs get as input already. How to actually go from experience to that intermediate layer without always having humans in the loop - that's an unsolved question. That's why I still find RL at this lower level exciting, how to make it as data efficient as possible. It has a lot to do with clever search and understanding what data you need.

Q: Do you imagine the brain has a single reward function? Are you searching for a holy grail in your work - a single reward function that scales to any sensor and actuator?

A: I would love to have an answer to that. I'm really interested if there's a single reward function or five that drive us as humans. That would be fascinating research because if you understand this, you really have an autonomous system with its own motivation that can self-improve like we do. I'm convinced we have something clever because humanity has made a lot of progress, and this was because individuals had this reward function. They wanted to contribute to science, improve others' lives. Of course, others just want money or land. But there's something intrinsic that drives us forward. It's a fascinating philosophical and algorithmic question. On the other side, this needs to be approached carefully and with AI safety and alignment in mind: if we understood this and had technical systems with the same free will as we have, it will be difficult or impossible to predict what these systems could achieve.

Q: It's fascinating that we have reached a period where we're asking these questions not philosophically, but practically before we even understand our own reward function and sub reward functions and how that works. But, anyway, that's getting into the realm of deep speculation now. Coming into 2025, what should we expect from you next?

A: What I want to push more is understanding how we can leverage LLMs to improve the reinforcement learning process. Instead of what most people are doing now with O1 or O3, using reinforcement learning to improve LLMs, we're looking at how to use LLMs to improve reinforcement learning for these lower levels. I'd love to see more research from us to understand this exploration process better, to push the boundary of what we can learn directly on robots without simulations. Because we're really so data efficient that the real robot can learn in real time and come to a reasonable end within what you'd expect from a human timeframe - a new task, and after a week you can perfectly manipulate that task.

Bye. x.
