Carbon Nanotubes in the Datacentre

A wonder material to solve all your datacentre thermal problems?

I’m republishing here because… well, datacentres. A place well we put a ton of servers on top of each other and next to each other and connect them. Everyone is an expert now in deprecation times and thermal envelopes though. Our future basically relies on depreciation calculations at this point. Life comes at you fast, I guess.

But while all the noobs are talking about co-packaged optics and SiPho, what comes after that? What if I told you, we have a material that has these properties:

  • Strength: 300x stronger than steel by weight.

  • Stiffness: Don’t deform under load, making them ideal for reinforcing other materials.

  • Heat conduction: Carry heat better than copper or even diamond, especially along the length of the tube.

  • Electrical conductivity: Similar to metals, but much lighter and more flexible.

  • Weight: They’re lighter than aluminum, despite delivering far better performance.

You would quite rightly ask: can I invest? Well hold your horses.

TLDR (just read this to get the gist and Skip to section V. CNT opportunities)

So the hyperscalers: your Googles, your Amazons, your Microsofts and your Metas (is Meta a hyperscaler?), are spending over $100 billion this year on datacentre stuff. Bubble or not, we should talk about what they’re building. NVIDIA’s GB200 NVL72 system is a liquid-cooled rack with 72 GPUs that draws 120 kW. A decade ago, a typical rack drew 10 kW. Today’s AI clusters pull 100–300 kW per rack. 10x jump in power density, and it creates problems:

  • Power delivery: When AI workloads ramp up, your voltage drops. Drop too low and it crashes training runs that cost hundreds of thousands in compute time.

  • Heat: 120 kW generates enormous heat in the same footprint. Some AI clusters spend 40% of total power just on cooling. You’re burning electricity to remove heat from burning electricity.

  • Interconnects: Moving data between tens of thousands of GPUs requires massive bandwidth. On-chip copper fights rising resistivity and electromigration (metal atoms migrating under current stress). Denser chip packaging needs bonds that survive thermal cycling. Facility cabling adds weight and resistive losses. Problems at every scale.

  • Optics: Datacentres are shifting to optical interconnects at shorter distances. (Kinda). But optical transceivers at 800 Gbps+ generate substantial heat in compact packages while maintaining sub-micron alignment.

The entire stack, from chip to cooling, hits physical limits simultaneously. Lots of solutions:

  • new logic (photonic, analog, neuromorphic, reversible)

  • new materials (graphene, 2D materials, diamond substrates)

  • new cooling (immersion cooling, two-phase systems, microfluidics)

  • new interconnects (optical I/O, wireless chip-to-chip, superconducting)

But as per the title, today I’m here to talk about carbon nanotubes. Here’s what you need to know:

  • Thermal interfaces, battery additives, and structural composites are production-ready now. So we are already seeing CNT in the datacentre.

  • Liquid cooling infrastructure is the 2-4 year opportunity as AI shifts to direct liquid cooling

  • On-chip interconnects are 5+ years out on semiconductor timelines but I have a 10+1+1 fund over here, so that works…

  • Markets range from hundreds of millions (thermal, batteries) to potentially billions (liquid cooling) to niche plays worth tens of millions (sensors). But collectively there is an opportunity for a CNT player to combine applications and become the de facto CNT winners across the datacentre.

I. Heat density and thermal reliability

So the thermal crisis starts at the chip and spreads outward. As rack power stepped from 10 kW to 100–300 kW, thermal interface materials began failing. Greases pump out. Pads age and lose compliance, and contact resistance increases. Eventually processors throttle to prevent damage. The result is that cooling infrastructure now consumes large fractions of site energy, with some facilities spending nearly half their power budget removing heat rather than performing computation. You’re paying for cooling, not computing. That’s the line.

Die-level thermal interfaces

CNT thermal interface pads work fit between chips and cooling systems, replacing degrading greases with solid materials that install dry and maintain stable heat transfer for years. At the microscopic level, even smooth surfaces have peaks and valleys. So when you press two surfaces together, the chip and cooling plate, they only touch at the peaks. Tiny air gaps everywhere else. Air is sub-optical/bad at conducting heat, so these gaps create thermal resistance. That’s your problem.

CNT thermal interfaces have millions of tiny carbon nanotube “bristles” that fill those gaps. The tubes are small and flexible enough to conform to microscopic irregularities, dramatically increasing actual contact area between hot chip and cooling system. More contact means better heat transfer. Plus you eliminate repeated thermal paste maintenance.

Conventional thermal paste costs c.$2 per CPU socket but needs replacement every 18–24 months as it degrades. Each maintenance cycle means technician time, logistics, etc. CNT thermal pads cost c.$8 per socket but last the server lifetime with no performance degradation. Over five years, conventional approaches cost $6–8 per socket in material and labour while CNT costs $8 once. At 100,000 servers with two sockets each, that’s $400K in direct savings. More importantly, you eliminate 100,000+ maintenance events that create downtime risk and configuration errors.

Facility structural composites

Facility components like ducting, fan blades, cable trays, equipment racks, raised floor tiles, seismic bracing, benefit from CNT-reinforced polymers at low loadings. The value prop is weight reduction, vibration damping, and protection against static discharge.

This is a volume play. Modest per-unit gains multiplied across thousands of components. A datacentre contains kilometres of cable tray and thousands of floor tiles. Weight reduction translates to shipping costs, installation labour, and reduced structural load on buildings. It all adds up.

Advanced packaging and networking hardware

The next frontier is advanced packaging where chips stack vertically or connect as modules. CNT-based connections using tiny pillar structures and textured films increase contact area while allowing lower-temperature bonding. The goal is simultaneous improvement in electrical conductivity and heat transfer, with materials that flex. The challenge is proving these work reliably across entire wafers and survive repeated heating and cooling cycles. This work is happening in labs now, so expect deployment in roughly three to five years.

Networking hardware like switches, routers, NICs, top-of-rack equipment, have similar thermal challenges at different scales. CNT thermal interfaces, heatsinks with CNT-enhanced base plates, and thermally conductive gap fillers all apply, but qualification paths differ from CPU/GPU cooling.

So at the end of all that, and I know it’s dense, here’s where we’ve ended up:

So look: chip-level thermal interfaces are production-ready with multiple suppliers, offering clear cost advantages over greases. Facility structural composites can be valuable but only at high volume. And advanced packaging connections are in development for three-to-five-year deployment, targeting denser chip stacking.

II. Power delivery and reliability

Okay so now on to power challenges. At the component level, AI workloads create spikes causing voltage instability. At rack level, capacitors positioned close to processors smooth power fluctuations and must maintain performance over years. At facility level, backup battery systems must recharge quickly and last for years because brief power interruptions terminate training runs costing hundreds of thousands in lost compute time.

Facility scale

Uninterruptible Power Supply (UPS) batteries with CNT additives are now standard in energy storage. Lower resistive losses and better mechanical integrity mean faster recharge and longer service intervals. Multiple major battery suppliers (LG Chem among others) have adopted CNT additives. You can specify this today. We can chalk this one up to a win already.

Another solid application is electromagnetic interference and static discharge components (EMI/ESD) built with CNT-loaded polymers. They, too, are qualified and production-ready. Stable shielding without brittleness and weight of metal flakes.

Component and rack scale

At the rack level we have thin-film CNT power storage devices placed close to processors to release energy during sudden demand spikes which prevents voltage drops that crash systems. This is still, however, in pilot territory: safety documentation and lifetime testing matter as much as electrical performance. Testing should show whether CNT solutions offer sufficient improvement over standard capacitors, optimized circuit board designs, and embedded capacitors to justify adoption.

Flexible CNT sensors offer monitoring for strain, humidity, airflow, and corrosive gases. These can be printed onto surfaces or integrated into cable runs. The value is in detecting coolant leaks before equipment damage, monitoring airflow to optimize cooling, identifying corrosive conditions before failures. The obvious challenge is connecting sensors to building management systems and showing calibration stability over years. So:

Battery additives and EMI shielding are done deals. Component-level storage and sensors are still in pilots and have to prove the performance gains are worth it.

III. Interconnect, packaging, and signal integrity

Interconnects, the wiring carrying signals and power, face challenges at every scale. Inside chips, copper wires shrink to such narrow widths that electrical resistance increases and metal atoms migrate under current stress, eventually causing failures, especially at higher signal frequencies. In chip packaging, manufacturers need denser connections at lower bonding temperatures to avoid damaging delicate components. At facility scale, kilometres of copper cabling add weight, cost, and energy losses.

Multiple approaches compete to solve these interconnect challenges. Photonic startups like Ncodin are pursuing optical I/O to bypass electrical limitations entirely at the chip-to-chip level. Others explore alternative conductors like graphene or novel architectures that reduce interconnect stress. CNTs compete with all of these on cost and manufacturability

The interconnect challenge spans from nanometre features inside chips to building-scale cable runs. Nine orders of magnitude?!

Production-ready

Static-safe handling and electromagnetic interference shielding using CNT composites reduce intermittent errors and ease service through weight reduction. Standard specifiable products competing against metal-loaded polymers. You can buy these.

CNT-enhanced cable jackets improve fire resistance, meeting building code requirements for flame spread and smoke generation. Straightforward material substitutions where CNT properties offer incremental advantages.

Pilot-ready

Copper-plus-CNT on-chip interconnect using CNT liners or partial fills targets current-carrying capacity and electromigration resistance in power rails and upper metal layers. This is foundry work, not datacentre operator decisions. But benefits from lower power consumption, higher reliability, and higher clock speeds flow through to deployed hardware. The challenges are process compatibility and long-term reliability at wafer scale.

Competing approaches include continued copper refinement, alternative materials like ruthenium, and architectural changes that reduce interconnect stress. Expect movement on semiconductor timelines: development work now appears in production three to five years later.

Low-temperature CNT interconnects for chiplets and 3D integration offer combined thermal and electrical advantages. If contact resistance and fatigue performance hold across wafers through thermal cycling, this transitions from research to packaging standard. Same timeline: decisions made by packaging engineers at semiconductor companies, with datacentre operators benefiting years downstream.

CNT fibres in cable harnesses present an honest trade-off. Mechanically superb—fatigue-proof, lightweight, high strength. Electrically? Commercial fibres achieve ~13–18% of copper conductivity. Development programs show ~61% compared to copper with promising results on the horizon. The milestone: credible kilometre-scale production at 70–80% of copper conductivity with batch-to-batch consistency.

Below this threshold, heat saved through weight reduction gets added back as resistive heating. Current CNT fibres make sense where flex cycle life and vibration resistance matter more than conductivity—robotic systems or equipment subject to continuous movement. For static facility-scale cabling? The volumetric opportunity is enormous but the conductivity gap remains prohibitive. Track development but don’t expect broad deployment until conductivity improves substantially.

Long-horizon

CNT logic devices could collapse the interconnect hierarchy, shorter power paths, simpler distribution, if you can combine low-power switching with building logic layers directly on top of each other. On-chip CNT optoelectronics could make short-reach electro-optic conversion a single process step rather than marrying two fabrication facilities.

Both remain research topics with barriers in materials purity, chirality control, alignment, and contamination management at wafer scale. The most prominent CNT startup, C12 Quantum Electronics in France, is working with individual nanotubes as qubits for quantum computing rather than classical logic: different application entirely. For classical CNT computing, work remains primarily in academic labs at MIT, Stanford, and Peking University. You want to watch these, breakthroughs would be massive, but they’re decades from deployment afaick.

One competitive note: glass-based photonics companies like Ephos, Absolics and Schott are doing on-chip connectivity through glass substrates rather than silicon photonics or CNT. Their pitch is better waveguide performance with lower propagation losses and simpler manufacturing. As glass core substrates gain traction in advanced packaging, they could become natural photonic networks for chip-to-chip connectivity. CNT photonics competes against both mature silicon photonics and emerging glass substrate approaches already manufactured at scale for packaging. Different paths to the same destination.

IV. Cooling systems and fluid management

So we’ve covered thermal interfaces at the chip level, power delivery from component to facility scale, and interconnects spanning nine orders of magnitude. Now we get to cooling: the system that has to remove all that heat we’ve been talking about.

Datacentres are shifting from air to direct liquid cooling and immersion cooling for high-density AI workloads. This brings new infrastructure: cold plates and manifolds, rear-door heat exchangers, immersion tanks, coolant distribution units, and associated pumping and heat rejection equipment. Even air-cooled facilities rely on massive HVAC: computer room air handlers, chillers, and air distribution systems.

Air cooling: production-ready applications

CNT composites in air handling like fan blades, impellers, ducting, offer weight reduction and vibration damping. Lighter fan assemblies reduce bearing loads and motor power. Straightforward qualification path: demonstrate equivalent strength, fatigue life, and fire safety versus incumbents.

CNT-enhanced air filters offer potential advantages in pressure drop (reducing fan power), particulate capture efficiency, or extended service intervals. These compete against conventional high-efficiency particulate filters on total cost of ownership: does improved performance or longer life justify premium pricing?

Liquid cooling: 2-4 year opportunity

Direct liquid cooling infrastructure is the next wave. The transition to liquid cooling for AI creates a chance to specify new materials during initial deployment rather than displacing entrenched solutions in mature systems. This timing advantage matters and the qualification burden is the same either way, but adoption friction is lower in new infrastructure versus retrofit. You’re building from scratch, so you can spec what you want.

CNT coatings on cold plates and heat exchangers target improved heat transfer and corrosion resistance. Testing must include thermal performance versus pressure drop, long-term corrosion in representative coolants (often containing corrosive additives), and fouling resistance (does coating accumulate deposits degrading performance over time?). CNT composite cold plates or manifolds might enable complex geometries through additive manufacturing or offer weight advantages, but must match thermal performance of machined copper or aluminium. High bar.

CNT-enhanced coolant fluids are complex. Nanofluids with CNT dispersions show improved thermal conductivity and heat transfer in labs. Barriers are long-term dispersion stability (do CNTs settle or agglomerate?), pump and seal compatibility (abrasion, chemical issues), fouling potential, and environmental health and safety qualification. Testing requires accelerated aging, material compatibility across all coolant-contact components, and realistic flow loops over thousands of hours. Think Cooligy Inc. This needs corporate EHS review and isn’t an easy replacement. Payoff could be big if testing works out, but early movers have to suffer large development costs and regulatory risk.

Water quality monitoring sensors based on CNT materials could detect contaminants, biological growth, or coolant degradation earlier. Leak detection sensors could identify coolant leaks before equipment damage. Same pattern as other CNT sensors, where the value is early warning and conformal installation, and the challenge is qualification and integration.

And there you have it. Air handling components you can buy now, liquid cooling infrastructure as the 2-4 year bet, and enhanced coolant fluids as the high-risk/high-reward option. Now let’s step back.

V. CNT opportunities

That’s a lot of applications. Sorry. The brief said “comprehensive”. So what do you do with all this? Some are production-ready but address small markets. Others are multi-billion-dollar opportunities decades away. Let’s sort by what matters: opportunity size and timing.

Large opportunities, production-ready (hundreds of millions annually)

Thermal interface materials, battery additives, and structural composites have crossed the qualification threshold and compete in established markets with multiple suppliers. Thermal interfaces address every CPU and GPU deployed—hundreds of millions annually growing with compute density. Battery additives are standard in UPS systems. Major suppliers like LG Chem use CNT materials in production volumes. Structural composites capture value through sheer quantity: every datacentre contains thousands of cable trays, floor tiles, racks, and HVAC components where modest per-unit improvements multiply.

These markets share characteristics: qualification complete, supply chains exist, procurement straightforward, growth from market expansion not technology displacement. Multiple suppliers compete on price and performance rather than convincing customers CNT materials work.

For investors: steady growth opportunities with predictable margins in competitive markets.

Large opportunities, 2-4 year horizon (potentially billions)

Liquid cooling infrastructure represents the next major market as some hyperscalers transition from air to liquid for high-density AI (while others have significantly improved the air-to-air cooling systems, think Thermal Works Inc). CNT coatings on cold plates and heat exchangers, CNT composite manifolds and distribution systems, and potentially CNT-enhanced coolant fluids address a market that could reach billions annually as liquid cooling becomes standard. Timing advantage is substantial: specifying CNT materials during initial deployment avoids friction of displacing working systems in mature installations. You’re building new infrastructure anyway, so you can spec CNT from the start.

High-speed optical module thermal management follow similar logic. As transceivers move to 800 Gbps+, thermal challenges get worse. CNT thermal solutions could capture hundreds of millions in annual revenue if qualification demonstrates advantages sufficient to justify requalification. These are markets where early movers bear R&D costs and regulatory risk but could capture first-mover advantages if pilot testing validates performance. Early bird gets the worm, but also bears the cost.

Large opportunities, 5+ year horizon (potentially billions to tens of billions)

On-chip interconnects, advanced packaging, and CNT photonics represent enormous markets if fundamental barriers are cleared. On-chip Cu+CNT interconnects could address a billion-dollar annual opportunity if semiconductor fabs demonstrate CNT liners or fills offer process-compatible solutions to electromigration and current-carrying limits. Advanced packaging interconnects for chiplets and 3D stacking could reach hundreds of millions as industry shifts toward disaggregated architectures.

The truly transformative opportunities—CNT logic enabling monolithic 3D integration, CNT photonics replacing hybrid integration, CNT fibres replacing copper in facility-scale cabling—could be multi-billion to tens-of-billions if materials science and process integration challenges are solved. But these are big ifs. CNT logic competes against a semiconductor industry with trillions in sunk capital. CNT photonics competes against silicon photonics platforms with decades of development plus emerging glass-based approaches. CNT cabling competes against centuries of copper manufacturing optimization. These aren’t easy fights.

Five-plus-year opportunities for on-chip applications and decade-plus (if ever) for architectural changes. Obviously you want to watch these because the opportunity is huge, but they’re not near-term yet. You’re betting on fundamental breakthroughs, not incremental improvements.

Niche opportunities, various timelines (tens of millions)

Specialized sensors (water quality, leak detection, gas monitoring), point-of-load power storage, specific EMI/ESD components, and CNT fibres in robotic systems address real problems but in markets worth tens of millions rather than billions. These remain viable businesses—tens of millions in annual revenue supports profitable companies—but won’t move market needles for large materials suppliers or create substantial investor returns in CNT-focused vehicles. Small but real.

What this means

CNT adoption will follow opportunity size. The hundreds-of-millions-annually markets are expanding now because qualification is complete and we have multiple suppliers competing. You can buy thermal interfaces, battery additives, and structural composites today from established vendors.

The potentially-billions markets depend on build-out timing. Liquid cooling deployments create specification windows over the next 2-4 years where CNT materials avoid retrofit friction. If you’re building new liquid cooling for AI clusters, you can specify CNT-coated cold plates or CNT composite manifolds without displacing working systems. Economics work differently for new build versus retrofit.

The tens-of-billions markets require either semiconductor industry adoption (5+ years) or fundamental breakthroughs in materials properties (decade-plus if ever). On-chip interconnects need process compatibility at wafer scale. And facility-scale CNT cabling needs conductivity to reach 70-80% of copper with kilometre-scale consistency. CNT photonics needs to compete with silicon photonics ecosystems and emerging glass substrate approaches that have massive head starts. These are long shots but with huge payoffs.

Niche applications will find customers regardless but represent small revenue pools that don’t justify dedicated investment vehicles. Real businesses, just small ones.

  • Near-term opportunities are where established markets meet steady growth. Thermal interfaces at $100M+ annually, battery additives at $200M+, structural materials at $100M+. And where infrastructure build-out creates specification windows—liquid cooling potentially reaching billions as AI deployments scale.

  • Longer-term opportunities are where process integration succeeds (on-chip interconnects), where materials science clears conductivity thresholds (facility cabling), or where photonics integration challenges solve (CNT optoelectronics). These are 5+ years out and depend on things beyond CNT supplier execution.

  • The pattern is CNTs expanding from proven applications into adjacent markets as more stuff gets qualified. This is not some moment where CNTs suddenly take over datacentres. It’s steady growth over 5-10 years, with the deepest integration questions remaining open beyond that horizon. Gradual, not revolutionary.

  • So yes, hyperscalers are spending $100 billion on datacentre infrastructure. A fraction—hundreds of millions now, potentially billions over the next decade—will flow to CNT materials solving these thermal, power, and interconnect challenges.
