Essay 10 Sep 2025

UK Opportunity in AI: Compound Semiconductors

Strategic leverage, not for fun and not for profit

The UK man. Hell of a job we’ve got on our hands. Just move to Spain, Amar says. Can’t mate, committed to a school down in Sussex, I say. Certainly would be easier though.

But nothing worth doing is easy. So here’s a question: how can the UK become a leader in AI? Don’t laugh at the back. We weren’t that far away. In a many-worlds interpretation we have ARM, Graphcore and Deepmind, and are the second/third most important country in the AI race.

So what do we think? Can we?

Takeways:

  • Compound-semi and photonics will become very important to AI stack

  • UK has a startegic advantage in these technologies

  • If we support the industry we can become a leader and use lead as strategic leverage

I. The Conventional Wisdom

First, the obvious answers.

Governance and diplomacy. The AI Security Institute supposedly gives the UK agenda-setting power on evals and standards. We led the International AI Safety Report process. An opportunity to "set the rules," they say. This smells awfully like the Good Chap Theory of Geopolitics in an age of realpolitik. Fine, I guess, but hardly leverage when the chips are down.

Second, sovereign compute. The UK has a national AI supercomputer called Isambard-AI, which went live in July 2025. Twenty-one "AI" exaflops in FP8. We have a Compute Roadmap to make this twenty times bigger by 2030. Big numbers so that’s good. This is a Good Thing, and we should build it. Build it bigger, why not? But what happens when Nvidia or Broadcom are forced to embargo us because we support Denmark at the UN Security Countil when the US takes Greenland by force? Oh, we’ve already got the chips and networking equipment so we win? False. What about updates and security? See Trident. IMO, no such thing as sovereign compute. If you disagree, you are wrong.

Third, NHS data. We have loads of high-quality data, and AI likes data. Well in the pre-training regime. Less so now with RL, but still. NHS data and AI. Peanut butter and jelly, yes? Well, privacy concerns and all that. So we have Secure Data Environments and the Federated Data Platform that create lawful, auditable access for research and operations. Pretty strong, maybe access to this dataset encourages health companies to set up shop here. It’s a good asset no doubt. Let’s put this in the “strong asset” pile.

Fourth, so-called cross-border data flows. This one feels weak as hell honestly. We have EU adequacy that runs to December 27, 2025 with renewal proposed. The UK-US "data bridge" has been in force since October 2023. But EU members have intra-EU flows and the EU-US Data Privacy Framework. Canada, Japan, and Korea also enjoy adequacy-like paths. This is at best a hygiene factor, not leverage. Also, as an aside, fully homomorphic encryption solves this in under five years, tops. See 2021 me on “collaborative computing”.

II. The Real Game: Supply Chain Chokepoints

Honestly, even if we scale “sovereign compute”, utilise NHS data and materially reduce the cost of electricity, we are missing a trick.

One word: semiconductors. Two words: compound semiconductors. Three words: compound semiconductors and photonics.

All in, it's actually compound semiconductors like Gallium Nitride, Silicon Carbide, Gallium Arsenide, and Indium Phosphide, plus photonics including III-V and silicon photonics with optical packaging. But obviously, that’s a mouthful. Let’s stick with compound-semi.

Now, first thing you need to know: forget strategic autonomy in AI. That’s for presidental speeches and US VCs talking their book. No country on Earth can have sovereign AI. Not even China, and by god they are trying. And in the US? Oh, you have Nvidia, a TSMC fab, and Broadcom, do you? But who's your neon guy? Who’s your photolith guy? Exactly.

Instead, be the only country in the world that can do X. Make X critical to the AI tech stack. Then say: you only get X if we get Y. That's geopolitics. You’re welcome.

And for the U.K: “X” is compound semiconductors and photonics.

III. Why Compound Semiconductors Matter

Silicon is good stuff, but it has fundamental limitations that become critical as AI scales.

Silicon can't make light efficiently. It struggles to convert electricity into light because of how its atoms are arranged. When you try to make silicon glow, most of the energy turns into heat instead of light. This is why your phone's LED lights and camera flash aren't made from silicon. For that, we use Gallium Arsenide.

Silicon is too slow for very high frequencies. While it works fine for regular computer processors, it can't handle the super-fast signals needed for cell phone towers, satellite communication, or radar systems. The electrons in silicon just don't move fast enough. So we use Indium Phosphide.

Silicon also breaks down in harsh conditions. It stops working properly when it gets too hot, faces intense radiation, or operates at very high power levels. This makes it unsuitable for electric car power systems, space satellites, or military equipment. Enter Gallium Nitride.

I mean it’s more complicated than that, but I mean it’s also a decent rule of thumb to say: we use compound semiconductors when we need to use light, operate at high frequencies, or work in harsh conditions.

IV: Photonics

Photonics uses light beams instead of electrical wires to carry information and do computing. We already use photonics extensively to move data with fiber optics where light's speed advantage overcomes the efficiency loss of converting electricity to light and back.

Every time you convert electrical signals to light using lasers and light back to electrical signals using detectors, you lose energy and add complexity. Over short distances, this conversion cost is bigger than any benefit you get from light's speed. But this is changing.

As AI chips need to move more data faster, and as we get better at making efficient converters, photonics is starting to make sense for connections between processors and even inside individual chips. The "break-even distance" where light becomes worth it keeps getting shorter as the technology improves.

Data centers are the next frontier where photonics is expanding, starting with connections between server racks and eventually moving to connections between individual chips.

III-V photonics uses compound semiconductors like InP and GaAs to make lasers, light detectors, and other components that generate and control light beams. Optical packaging is the art of connecting these tiny light-based components together with fiber optic cables and making sure the light goes exactly where it needs to go without leaking or getting lost.

Together, these technologies will become the backbone of super-fast internet, AI data centers that compute with light, and communication systems that work at light speed rather than the slower speed of electrons moving through copper wires.

IV. *Not* The End of History

It's close to inevitable that we will use more compound semiconductors over the next decade as we run computers faster and hotter, and use more photons instead of electrons.

  1. Optical Interconnects Will Replace Copper

Today, racks of Nvidia H100/H200 GPUs are linked mostly by copper. But copper tops out around 400G reliably, while Nvidia's next-gen AI clusters already push 800G to 1.6T per port. At those speeds, copper wires turn into heating elements. The only way forward is silicon photonics plus compound semiconductors.

Indium Phosphide (InP) lasers and detectors plus GaAs modulators power the optical engines inside high-speed interconnect modules. Every future hyperscale GPU cluster from OpenAI, Google, and Amazon will need these to move petabytes per second between racks.

  1. GaN Power Electronics Will Be Mandatory

AI datacenters already burn tens of megawatts per site. Each rack can draw 30 to 60kW today; Nvidia's Blackwell clusters will hit over 80kW per rack. Silicon-based power converters waste too much energy as heat. Gallium Nitride (GaN) switches power far more efficiently and at higher frequencies.

GaN-based DC-DC converters are replacing silicon transistors (MOSFETs) as switching elements in GPU power delivery networks. GaN also powers server PSUs, liquid cooling pumps, and AI accelerator front-ends where efficiency is critical. Every AI cluster deployment plan from Amazon, Google, and Microsoft includes GaN migration by 2026 to 2027.

  1. Thermal Limits Will Force Photon-Based Compute

As we hit 2nm and below, silicon transistors face power density walls. Nvidia's H200 already draws around 700W; future Blackwell-class GPUs will hit 1kW each. That forces a shift toward photonics and heterogeneous integration through on-package photonics for ultra-low latency GPU-to-GPU links, and optical AI accelerators where InP/GaAs modulators handle AI matrix operations.

InP and GaAs photonic integrated circuits already outperform silicon photonics for high-speed, low-loss lasers and modulators. AI datacenter roadmaps from Nvidia, Broadcom, and Meta all assume integrated photonics by around 2027 to 2029.

  1. Sensors and Edge AI Will Demand Exotic Materials

And it’s not just datacentre scaling. We are slowly seeing the diffusion of AI “at the edge”. As AI expands into robotics, autonomous systems, and climate-scale IoT, high-performance sensors become critical. HgCdTe infrared sensors, InGaAs photodiodes, and other compound devices dominate for thermal imaging, LIDAR, and satellite vision. These sensors feed data into AI models faster and at higher fidelity than silicon-based alternatives can deliver.

Supply Chain Reality: Few Global Suppliers, Hard to Replace

Unlike silicon wafers, a highly commoditized market, compound semiconductors have fewer than ten scaled suppliers worldwide per material. For InP lasers: IQE, Sumitomo, Coherent, and Win Semi dominate. For GaN power wafers, it’s Wolfspeed, IQE, and Epiworld.

Switching suppliers is slow, expensive, and disruptive, giving those who control these nodes quiet leverage over AI infrastructure buildouts. Compound semiconductors become non-optional as AI scales because they move data faster through InP/GaAs lasers and modulators, deliver power more efficiently through GaN converters, handle heat and integration challenges through photonics and advanced packaging, and enable new sensing plus edge AI through InGaAs and HgCdTe detectors.

It’s not as simple as saying let’s become the TSMC or ASML of compound-semi and photonics. But it’s also not much more complex than that.

V. UK's Actual Leverage

Good, so one isn’t just “good” at the entire supply chain. What does the UK actually do well?

1. Compound-Semiconductor Epitaxy

The UK is good at compound-semiconductor epitaxy (crystal growth). We have IQE in Cardiff and the broader CSconnected cluster in South Wales. IQE is the largest independent epiwafer supplier globally, with roughly 60% share of the merchant InP/GaAs epitaxy market. Most other suppliers like Win Semi in Taiwan, Coherent/II-VI in the US, and Sumitomo Electric in Japan are either captive, serving their own fabs, or significantly smaller.

IQE's moat is technical and qualification-driven. Epitaxy involves growing ultra-pure crystalline layers, often just a few atoms thick, with extreme uniformity. Defect densities must stay below one per billion atoms in some cases. For high-speed photonics and power electronics, even slight material imperfections ruin yields. Once a device design is qualified on IQE's wafers, switching suppliers involves redesigning the stack and requalifying the entire product, which can take twelve to twenty-four months and millions of dollars.

This gives the UK real leverage in the AI hardware stack, because InP wafers are the foundation of photonic interconnects, the lasers, detectors, and modulators that allow GPUs in AI datacenters to communicate a faster speeds. There are fewer than five scaled suppliers globally, and IQE is one of them.

2. Compound-Semiconductor Deposition and Etch Tools

We don’t just grow compound semis; we also build the tools that deposit and etch the layers that become chips. Oxford Instruments Plasma Technology is a leading UK supplier of plasma etch and deposition systems for III-V devices, including InP photonics, GaN power, and GaAs RF. Mainstream silicon tools are dominated by giants like Applied Materials, Lam Research, and Tokyo Electron. Those vendors are less entrenched in compound-semiconductor processes, where Oxford has a defensible niche.

Oxford's moat lies in its process "recipes," the finely tuned plasma chemistries, gas flows, and atomic-layer parameters co-developed with device manufacturers. These recipes are deeply embedded in customers' high-value products, and switching to a different tool vendor often requires multi-quarter requalification. Oxford also benefits from close integration with European research hubs like Southampton ORC, Fraunhofer HHI, and imec, positioning it as the default partner for next-gen photonic interconnect and GaN power conversion R&D.

Oxford's customer base includes leading optical module makers, GaN foundries, and RF chip manufacturers globally, meaning the UK's influence scales into every datacenter and wireless network deploying AI infrastructure.

3. Lithography Encoders and Interferometers

In semiconductor manufacturing, each process step requires positioning silicon wafers with extraordinary accuracy. Lithography creates the circuit patterns by exposing the wafer to light through masks, while bonding fuses multiple wafers together, etching removes unwanted material, and testing verifies the finished chips. Every one of these operations demands that the wafer sits in exactly the right spot. For that you need optical encoders and laser interferometers. Think GPS for ASML machines.

The UK has a little FTSE250 company called Renishaw. It’s the market leader. Encoders and interferometers are co-designed with stage makers and scanner OEMs; swapping affects tuning, error maps, and throughput. Re-qualification is slow and risky. Renishaw has delivered bespoke differential interferometry for ASML, showing high-end design-in, but it's a competitive market. Germany's Heidenhain is a major design-in for lithography and front-end tools, and the interferometer market has multiple heavyweights, notably US-based Zygo and Keysight targeting wafer-stage position metrology.

The UK has a strong talent pipeline: Renishaw's apprenticeships and graduates, NPL's metrology depth, Huddersfield's Future Metrology Hub, Cranfield's ultra-precision programs, Southampton ORC/Zepler for photonics, plus nanopositioning know-how around Cambridge like Queensgate.

Being a leader isn’t just about building big models and datacentres. That’s a capex game we can’t win. Sure by all means build sovereign capabilities for worst-case scenario. But let’s play a game we can win.

VI. Policy Levers

Now by all means we can scream into the void and say we want more money and we need to “think bigger”, but let’s deal with politics as it is today shall we. If I were advising Number 10 on AI, it would go a little something like this:

  • 30% R&D tax credits. Increase the 20% expenditure credit to 30% or higher. Companies receive roughly 15-16% net benefit after corporation tax. A company spending £10 million on R&D books a £2 million credit and retains £1.5 million net. Raising the credit to 30% would deliver around 22.5% net benefit at the same tax rate. Loss-making, R&D-intensive SMEs already access up to 27% cash support under the Enhanced R&D Intensive Support scheme, providing a foundation for sector-specific enhancements.

  • 200% capital allowance. A 200% first-year capital allowance for advanced manufacturing equipment critical to the UK’s semiconductor and photonics supply chain. Today, permanent full expensing already allows companies to deduct 100% of the cost of new qualifying machinery in the year of purchase. Double the allowance to 200% for epitaxy reactors, MBE/MOCVD systems, advanced etch and deposition tools, etc and a £5 million investment would deliver a £10 million deduction against taxable profits. At the current rate, that’s a £2.5 million effective subsidy, halving the upfront cost of scaling production capacity.

  • Dedicated export finance support through UKEF would create a specialist pathway for compound semiconductor and photonics exports. UK Export Finance can already support up to 85% of an export contract's value and offers direct loans up to £200 million per transaction at OECD Commercial Interest Reference Rate-linked terms. For a £120 million production line, this represents up to £102 million in financing with just a 15% buyer down payment. UKEF operated with a £60 billion statutory exposure cap in 2024-25 and peaked at approximately £58.8 billion, leaving operational headroom to prioritize strategic sectors within existing capacity.

Seems small? Well good, that means we can do it and we don’t have to compete on the who can spend most money game with the US and China.

There is alot more work on the policy side and information I don’t have. These adjustments might not be possible for reasons I don’t know. But there are other tweaks I’m sure.

The takeaways are clear:

  • Compound-semi and photonics will become very important to AI stack

  • UK has a startegic advantage in these technologies

  • If we support the industry we can become a leader and use lead as strategic leverage
