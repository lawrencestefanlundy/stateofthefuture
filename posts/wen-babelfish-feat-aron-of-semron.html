Edge AI: Analog Chips (feat. Aron Kirschen, SEMRON)

ai inference will have to move to the edge, when it does, what chips will we use?

Edge AI is a thing. Sure we can build Stargate, but some of our AI will need to run locally on glasses, earphones, watches, smartphones, robots, cars, drones, etc, and et al.

But startups have been trying to do AI on small devices for a while. It always uses too much power or needs too much memory or compute power. Until now?

Maybe. Take a gander at analog computing:

Before we start, this interview contains strong language and scenes some readers might find upsetting. Lol. Obvs, not. But actually I do sort of jump in at the deep end. So quick primer:

  • Digital Computing: The standard approach used in CPUs, GPUs, and most AI accelerators. It represents data as binary values (0s and 1s) and processes it through logic gates. It’s precise and scalable.

  • Analog Computing: Uses continuous signals (like electrical charge, resistance, or magnetism) instead of binary values. It can perform certain computations, like multiplications and summations, directly in hardware without converting to digital. However, is suffers from noise and precision issues compared to digital approaches.

  • Why Memory Matters for Analog Computing: Unlike digital processors, which shuttle data between memory and logic units, analog computing benefits from storing and processing data within the same physical medium (e.g., charge, resistance, or magnetism). Memory cells in certain materials can naturally perform operations like accumulation and multiplication, making memory-centric computing a natural fit for analog acceleration.

  • Compute-in-Memory (CIM) In both digital and analog, data is constantly moved between the processor and memory, which consumes most of the power and slows down performance. CIM addressed this by performing computations inside the memory, reducing data movement and boosting efficiency. CIM can be implemented:

    • Digital CIM: Uses binary logic (0s and 1s) inside memory to perform operations. More precise but less energy-efficient.

    • Analog CIM: Uses continuous physical properties (like charge, resistance, or magnetism) to process data inside memory. It’s much more efficient for AI workloads but can have precision challenges.

  • CapRAM, Resistive RAM (ReRAM or RRAM), and Magnetoresistive RAM (MRAM) are distinct memory technologies, each utilizing a different physical mechanism for data storage and, in some cases, in-memory processing.

    • CapRAM (Capacitive RAM) → Analog CIM

      • Stores and computes using electrical charge (capacitance).

      • Uses standard materials, making it easier to scale.

      • Promises higher density and better energy efficiency than alternatives.

    • RRAM (Resistive RAM) → Analog CIM

      • Uses resistance changes to store and process data.

      • Can suffer from variability and retention issues, making it harder to scale.

    • MRAM (Magnetoresistive RAM) → Analog or Digital CIM

      • Uses magnetic states to store and process data.

      • More stable than RRAM but requires exotic materials, which limits foundry adoption.

So what do we all need to remember? That analog computing can dramatically improve energy efficiency, but it requires memory technologies that can natively store and process analog data.

Yes? Very good, onwards.

Takeaways

  1. Edge AI Needs Compute That Scales Efficiently: The primary constraint for edge AI is energy efficiency, driven by the cost of moving data between memory and compute units. Most likely winning architecture will co-locate processing with storage while maintaining high parameter density and manufacturability for scalability.

  2. CapRAM’s Standard Materials Could Give It an Edge: Unlike RRAM and MRAM, which require exotic materials and specialized fabrication steps, CapRAM leverages existing CMOS processes. This eliminates major retooling costs and enables easier integration into established semiconductor manufacturing, potentially accelerating CIM commercialization.

  3. Energy Efficiency + 3D Stacking = CapRAM’s Big Bet: CapRAM enables vertical integration without the scaling limitations of SRAM or the high lithography costs of 3D RRAM. By performing in-memory computation in an analog domain, it reduces on-chip data movement, offering higher compute density per watt—a critical factor for edge inference workloads.

SEMRON builds analog chips using a novel 3D architecture called CapRAM. They claim CapRAM™ technology delivers (checks notes: says here “unmatched”) compute-in-memory power, significantly boosting performance for AI-driven devices with 0.2-1 T OPS/mW* the highest energy efficiency in the world and 500 M PARAMS/mm² achieving the highest parameter density in the world.

They claim the industry is doing it wrong. Aron is nothing if not competitive.

Aron, mate, how do different compute-in-memory approaches compare for edge AI deployment, considering manufacturing scalability, process integration, and cost? What unique advantages or challenges does capacitive storage present versus resistive or magnetic alternatives?

That’s a really important question. If we look at MRAM and RRAM, both of them require introducing new materials into the manufacturing process, which foundries generally dislike. If you do decide to use those materials, you end up completely dependent on whichever foundry supports them. Capacitive storage, or “CapRAM,” is different because it only needs process development with existing materials—no exotic additions. That makes it potentially more scalable and cost-effective to manufacture.

What are the key manufacturing and device physics challenges in implementing variable capacitance for weight storage compared to memristive approaches? Consider aspects like process variability, state retention, and scaling limitations in CMOS-compatible fabrication processes.

Variability can be an issue for both MRAM and RRAM, and while MRAM can use spintronic approaches to mitigate it, those aren’t very energy-efficient. Capacitive approaches tend to be more energy-efficient overall, partly because their noise characteristics are better. In addition, most emerging memory technologies have big problems with variability/low precision, which is a killer if we talk about analog in-memory computing for modern AI models. As for retention, we’ve shown that six bits can be stored reliably for ten years, which is more than enough for most inference workloads right now. Endurance is also good: above ten thousand cycles for CapRAM, which covers typical inference needs. When it comes to 3D stacking, MRAM and RRAM likely won’t scale monolithically for at least a decade without becoming prohibitively expensive, so they aren’t ideal for anything beyond very simple edge tasks. Even SRAM isn’t expected to be fully 3D-scale-ready until somewhere around 2040, so there’s a real cost barrier there.

What workloads or use cases might be poorly suited for a 3D CapRAM architecture, and how might these limitations affect market positioning? I’m thinking dynamic range requirements, update frequency, and computational precision needs.

Honestly, I don’t see many workloads that 3D CapRAM couldn’t handle. Unlike RRAM-based approaches, CapRAM can function in both a non-volatile mode (for things like convolutional layers) and a volatile mode (for attention layers in Transformers). The update frequency—on the order of milliseconds for the non-volatile operations and a few 100ns for the volatile operations—is plenty for most inference tasks. Dynamic range and precision are fine as is, and can be expanded if needed.

How will the shift to smaller models and inference workloads on edge devices using time-test compute change hardware design? What is the best way to deal with test-time compute’s iterative refinement process and memory requirements for maintaining multiple candidate solutions?

I don’t really see smaller models as a major issue, because in a digital architecture you can just run many of them in parallel if you want. But in general, more parameters still mean smarter results, as long as you’ve got enough high-quality data to train on. I don’t believe we’ll ever hit a point where we say, “This is smart enough—no need to make it better.” Time-test or iterative compute does add complexity to inference, but if you have enough bandwidth for more tokens, there’s no reason not to use it for higher accuracy.

How might advancements in traditional digital architectures (like advanced packaging, chiplets, near-memory computing) affect the competitive landscape for analog computing approaches? What sustainable advantages could memcapacitive solutions maintain?

For edge devices, I just don’t see digital architectures hitting the tera-operations-per-second levels we need without burning too much power. There’s a thermal ceiling of around 75 mW per square centimeter, and once you pass that, it’s tough to manage heat. Sure, the industry keeps innovating, but those are mostly incremental improvements. CapRAM has an inherent energy-efficiency edge because it doesn’t spend the bulk of its power budget shuttling data back and forth. Non-von-Neumann digital or SRAM-based analog designs face serious cost challenges: wafer prices at advanced nodes are sky-high, and there’s no sign of that changing dramatically anytime soon.

To be direct: I think RRAM and SRAM for AI inference won’t cut it for broader, more demanding workloads. Anyone who invested heavily in them had all the facts but might have been steered by biased experts. One more note on 3D RRAM: besides materials and device issues, every layer requires lithography, which makes it really expensive and slow to scale. Throw in all the other complications, and it just isn’t shaping up to be a good solution going forward.

Debrief

If aron’s views turn out to be correct, the biggest implication would be a major shift in how the industry approaches compute-in-memory for edge AI. Today, many efforts focus on RRAM, MRAM, or trying to push digital architectures further, but if those hit insurmountable cost or performance walls, then capacitive storage (CapRAM) could take over as the default for large-scale edge inference. That would ripple throughout the supply chain—foundries, tool vendors, and device manufacturers might pivot R&D funding and production lines toward capacitive solutions and away from resistive or magnetic devices.

  1. Foundry Adoption

Because CapRAM relies on existing materials instead of exotic, specialized layers, more foundries could integrate it without huge capital expenditures. This could mean faster scalability and potentially lower end-device costs.

  1. Energy Efficiency Focus

Edge computing is all about hitting the right balance of power and performance. If CapRAM can significantly cut energy use by reducing data transfers (the “95% of power spent on moving data” argument), that helps unlock higher TOPS in edge devices without overheating or requiring exotic cooling.

  1. 3D Stacking and Density

If it’s true that MRAM and RRAM can’t scale monolithically in 3D anytime soon, but CapRAM can, that sets the stage for more dense, specialized AI memory arrays. Higher density at manageable cost could let edge devices handle workloads that previously required a data center.

Where He Might Be Wrong

  1. Underestimating Potential Breakthroughs in 3D RRAM/MRAM

Aron claims it will be at least a decade before 3D RRAM or MRAM see meaningful commercial traction. However, history in semiconductors often takes surprising turns:

  • Aggressive Industry Roadmaps: Major players like TSMC, Samsung, and Intel are pouring billions into advanced R&D. They have active programs for emerging memories (e.g., Everspin for MRAM, multiple academic/industrial consortia for RRAM). These companies have shown they can accelerate “10-year horizons” to half that time when there’s enough market pressure and capital.

  • Recent Demonstrations: Research groups have demonstrated multi-layer RRAM stacks in smaller prototypes. For instance, multiple IEEE papers show 3D RRAM arrays with modest densities, and the progress from lab to pilot line can be faster if a foundry sees a clear commercial upside

  • Parallel Development of 3D Integration: Through-silicon vias (TSVs), advanced packaging, and wafer stacking techniques are improving rapidly. If these integration approaches circumvent some of the lithography challenges, 3D resistive memory might not be as far-fetched as he thinks.

If 3D RRAM or MRAM achieve cost-effective scaling sooner than Aron predicts, the argument that CapRAM is the only path forward becomes much weaker. CapRAM might have great theoretical advantages, but a sudden leap in 3D RRAM or MRAM manufacturing could undercut its market adoption.

  1. Downplaying How Quickly Foundries Can Qualify Exotic Materials

Aron says foundries hate new materials and are unwilling to qualify them, but that may be an oversimplification:

  • Existing Roadmaps for Materials: Foundries already work with complex stacks (e.g., high-k dielectrics, low-k dielectrics, complex etch processes, metal gates). They’ve shown they can add new materials if there’s a huge demand (e.g., integrated silicon photonics, certain memory materials like hafnium oxides for RRAM).

  • Consolidation Trends: Because a few leading foundries (TSMC, Samsung, Intel) dominate advanced nodes, once one invests in a new material set, the others often follow to remain competitive. This can cascade quickly if a customer (e.g., Apple, AMD, Qualcomm) sees a performance advantage.

  • Embedded MRAM Success: TSMC already offers embedded MRAM at 22nm and 16nm, which suggests that foundries are willing to integrate these technologies into well-established nodes. If that success continues, the door is open for even more advanced foundry support, not less.

If the foundries see big customers for advanced RRAM/MRAM processes, they can bring them into mainstream production faster than expected. Aron’s assertion that CapRAM will remain simpler and cheaper hinges on the idea that foundries universally resist new materials—a stance that may not hold up if the business case justifies the adoption.

  1. Overlooking Specialized Market Segments and Alternative Technologies (e.g., Optoelectronics)

Aron dismisses RRAM/MRAM (and even SRAM) as “dead,” but that might overlook specialized markets and other non-capacitive alternatives:

  • High-Reliability / Low-Volume Sectors: Aerospace, defense, and certain automotive applications place a premium on radiation hardness, high reliability, or extremely low standby power—areas where MRAM, FRAM, or even older floating-gate solutions can excel, and cost sensitivity is less of a concern.

  • Optoelectronic Approaches: Companies like Lightmatter and Luminous Computing have showcased prototype photonic chips that aim for ultra-low latency and high bandwidth. While not necessarily competing with CapRAM for mainstream consumer edge devices, these solutions appeal to certain niche markets (e.g., latency-sensitive applications, specialized HPC). If a portion of the edge market decides that “low-latency at moderate-to-high power” is worth it, optoelectronic or hybrid photonic/electronic chips could flourish.

  • Heterogeneous Integration: AI edge devices can be a combination of digital, analog, optical, and specialized memory accelerators. Even if CapRAM is compelling, it might coexist with other modules specialized for different parts of the workload.

If niche or specialized segments remain profitable for MRAM, RRAM, or photonic approaches, those technologies aren’t truly “dead.” They can carve out a robust slice of the market where cost is secondary to performance or reliability. Meanwhile, breakthroughs in photonic computing for edge AI could create use cases that overshadow purely capacitive solutions in terms of raw latency and throughput—particularly if power usage isn’t the primary bottleneck.
